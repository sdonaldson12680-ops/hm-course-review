AIDET & Key Words at Key Times

You've used these hundreds of times.
Here's what they look like at their best.

AIDET Framework
A
Acknowledge Make immediate eye contact and greet by name

Greet every person immediately and warmly — before anything else happens. Use their name when you know it.

"Good morning, Mr. Johnson, I'm glad you're here. Let me get you settled right away."

"Good morning, Sarah, I’m glad you reached out. Let me pull that up and help you get started."

I
Introduce Your name, role, and experience

Tell patients who you are, what your role is, and why they should feel confident in your care.

"I'm Maria, and I'll be your nurse today. I've been with Houston Methodist for eight years and I'm here to make sure you're well cared for."

"Hi, I’m Maria from HR. I’ll be helping you through the onboarding process today, and I’m here to make sure you know what to expect every step of the way."

D
Duration Set time expectations clearly

Give patients and families a realistic timeframe so they're never left wondering what happens next.

"The procedure should take about 45 minutes. I'll check in with you every 15 minutes so you always know where things stand."

"This should take about 10 minutes, and I’ll keep you updated along the way so you always know what to expect."

E
Explain What you're doing and why it matters

Walk patients through each step in plain language. Anxiety drops when people understand what's happening to them.

"I'm going to take your blood pressure now — this helps us make sure everything is stable before we begin."

"I’m going to walk you through the next steps now so you know exactly what to expect and where to go from here."

T
Thank Close every interaction with gratitude

Thank patients for their trust, their patience, and for choosing Houston Methodist. End on a human note.

"Thank you for your patience today, Mr. Johnson. It's truly a privilege to take care of you."

"Thank you for your patience today, I really appreciate your flexibility, and I’m glad I could help."

Every interaction is a touchpoint — AIDET makes each one count.
Key Words at Key Times
Safe

Clinical: "I want you to know you are safe here. Our team is with you every step of the way."

Non-Clinical: "You’re in good hands — we’ll help you through this together."

Comfortable

Clinical: "Is there anything I can do to make you more comfortable right now?"

Non-Clinical: "Do you have everything you need before we get started?"

Private

Clinical: "Let me close the door so we have some privacy while we talk."

Non-Clinical: "Let’s step into a quieter space so we can discuss this privately."

Always

Clinical: "We will always keep you informed before we do anything. You’ll never be surprised."

Non-Clinical: "I’ll always keep you updated if timelines or next steps change."

My Pleasure

Clinical: "It’s my pleasure. I’m happy to help take care of that for you."

Non-Clinical: "My pleasure — let me know if there’s anything else you need."

Manage Up
Speak positively about your team — proactively.

Before handing someone off to another department, team member, or leader, tell them something genuine about the next person: "You're in great hands, Dr. Patel is one of the best, or Alex from payroll is fantastic to work with." It builds trust before the introduction even happens.

You've used AIDET hundreds of times. Maybe thousands. You Acknowledge. You Introduce yourself. You set Duration. You Explain. You Thank.

It's not a script, it's a rhythm. And when it's working, the person in front of you feels seen, safe, and informed.

On the right, Key Words at Key Times; the specific phrases that signal care in the moments that matter most: safe, comfortable, private, always, my pleasure.

Explore at your own pace. Nothing is locked here, just revisit what resonates.

On click — A · Acknowledge

Acknowledge sounds simple, but it sets the tone for everything that follows. Look up. Make eye contact. Use someone’s name if you know it. When people feel seen the moment an interaction begins, trust builds faster, conversations feel easier, and connection comes naturally. Don’t underestimate the first few seconds — they shape more than you think.

On click — I · Introduce

Introduce yourself by name, by role, and by experience. Whether you're helping a patient, supporting a coworker, or guiding a visitor, people want to know who they're interacting with and why they can trust you. A simple introduction creates confidence faster than almost anything else — so don’t skip it.

On click — D · Duration

Duration is about respect for someone else's time and uncertainty. "This will take about forty-five minutes." "I'll be back in fifteen." Anxiety lives in the gaps between what's happening and what someone thinks is happening. Close those gaps with a number. Even an estimate beats silence every time.

On click — E · Explain

Explain in plain language, not technical language. Tell people what’s happening, what to expect, and why it matters. Clear communication reduces confusion, builds confidence, and helps people feel included instead of uncertain. When people understand what’s happening around them, trust grows. When they don't, fear fills in the blanks — and the blanks are usually worse than the truth.

On click — T · Thank

Thank closes the loop. Thank them for their patience, for their trust, for choosing Houston Methodist. It costs you nothing and it lands every time. The thank-you isn't a formality — it's the moment that tells the patient this wasn't a transaction. It was care.

Key Words

Now the Key Words. Five small phrases that do a lot of quiet work.

Safe — whether it’s a patient preparing for a procedure or a coworker navigating a difficult situation, hearing the words “you’re safe here” lowers anxiety and builds trust almost instantly. Use it on purpose.

Comfortable — asking if someone is comfortable shows you’re paying attention to the person, not just the task in front of you. That might mean adjusting a patient’s pillow, or checking in with a new employee during orientation.

Private — privacy is dignity made visible. Sometimes that means closing a patient room door. Other times it means stepping into a quieter space before discussing sensitive information with a coworker or family member.

Always — “we will always keep you informed” turns one promise into a pattern. Whether you’re updating a patient, a department partner, or a leader waiting on information, consistency builds trust faster than almost anything else.

And my pleasure — one of the simplest phrases, and one people remember most. Whether you’re helping a patient, answering a question from a coworker, or supporting a visitor, it signals that helping others isn’t an inconvenience. It’s part of who we are.
